Emotions at Play: Designing AI & Tangible Tools for Children’s Emotional Learning

A participatory UX research and design project exploring how AI and playful physical tools can support children’s emotional learning. I led the full design cycle with 27 children (ages 6–12), from co-design sessions to Figma prototyping and iterative testing.

Problem Statement

  1. Context & Challenge:
    Children are under emotional stress (anxiety, isolation, peer tension) but have fewer safe spaces to work through it together. Research shows that play — storytelling, pretending, co-inventing rules — helps kids rehearse and regulate emotions socially.
  2. Gap in Existing Approaches:
    Most “emotional learning” tools are adult-led and individual (“tell me how you feel”). They miss how kids actually process feelings: joking about them, acting them out, and realizing “you felt that too.” Tech and AI tools rarely support that peer-to-peer emotional work.
  3. Our Response & Research Aim:
    We built and tested playful tools — physical and AI-powered — that help kids express emotions, see shared experiences, and create emotional artifacts they can discuss together. Our guiding question: Can we design technology that functions like a campfire — something kids gather around to tell real stories?
Research Goal
Understand how children naturally express difficult feelings with peers, translate those behaviors into design requirements, and prototype an AI-assisted tool that supports empathy — not just self-calming.

1 · Understand

I mapped common emotional “pressure points” for kids: embarrassment in public, conflict with friends, fear of being seen as “overreacting.” I also surveyed existing SEL-style tools.

The pattern: most tools try to correct behavior (“calm down, use the right feeling word”). But prior work shows kids process emotions together — by joking, acting it out, remixing the story — not by quietly self-reporting.

Design direction: We shouldn't just design for the individual child. We should design for the space between children.

2 · Observe (Participatory Design with Children)

PD1 · Storytelling Dice

We used oversized “emotion dice.” Kids rolled anger / fear / disgust / sadness and told real stories from their own lives. They illustrated and acted them out.

They loved the “negative” emotions. One participant declared that disgust was their favorite emotion. This showed us that difficult feelings can be playful and performable — not just “problems.”

PD2 · Empathy Game (“You Are Not Alone”)

Small groups made games where you admit a stressful moment (“I was nervous before speaking”) and others signal “me too.” Empathy sounded like “same, you’re not weird,” not “I’m sorry.”

Core insight:
Kids naturally created emotional tokens — drawings, cards, stickers — and treated them like badges of experience. They wanted something physical to point to and say, “This is how I felt.”

That behavior became the seed of the product direction.

[Insert PD1 / PD2 photos: dice game, empathy game boards, early handmade “feeling stickers”]

We learned kids want emotions to become artifacts they can keep, trade, and talk about.

3 · Define

We defined a core requirement:

Design requirement:
Give kids a playful, ownable emotional artifact — something they can edit, laugh about, gift to a friend, or keep for themselves. Not advice. A thing.

We turned our findings into three design pillars:

  • Curiosity – “What happens if I roll this?” “What happens if I regenerate?”
  • Ownership – “That’s not how I felt. I’ll change it.”
  • Enjoyment – Humor made honesty feel safe.

I then used Curiosity · Ownership · Enjoyment as a rubric for every UX decision.

[Insert affinity map / sticky notes labeled: Curiosity · Ownership · Enjoyment]

These three pillars became our “playful learning” criteria.

4 · Ideate

We proposed the AI Emotional Sticker Tool.

  • Child briefly describes a real emotional moment.
  • Tool generates a “feel-better” sticker (image + message).
  • Child edits the sticker or makes one for a friend.

This supports the campfire model: the sticker becomes the shared object in the middle. Kids can look at it together, react to it, laugh at it, correct it — without putting any one kid on the spot.

Design shift: AI is not “the expert.” AI is a creative partner that starts conversations.

5 · Prototype (AI Emotional Sticker Tool)

I built mid- and hi-fidelity Figma prototypes. The core flow:

  • Story Input: “Tell us what happened” + choose an emotion (sad / angry / scared / worried / lonely).
  • AI Output Preview: Sticker art + short supportive message + plain-language reflection.
  • Customize: Kids can rewrite the message, regenerate visuals, add fun elements (“make it cuter”).
  • Print & Share: Stickers export to print so kids can keep them, trade them, show them at home.

I deliberately avoided “teacher voice.” Kids distrusted prescriptive tone (“Calm down.”). They preferred messages that sounded like a caring peer or a mascot, which became a content design rule.

6 · Test

We tested two modes: “self-sticker” (for me) and “peer-sticker” (for you). Kids compared “how I described my feeling” vs “how you described my feeling.”

This led to safe emotional correction, like: “I wasn’t mad, I was scared.” They talked about the sticker, not about “what’s wrong with you.”

They also critiqued AI freely: “The AI’s message is weird.” “Message needs to be improved.” “I want it to be slow.”

And they loved the physical sticker. One said it “makes me feel happy,” especially if it had cute characters.

Design lesson: Kids don’t want perfect empathy from AI. They want editable empathy.

[Insert testing photos: kids comparing “my sticker” vs “your sticker of me,” laughing at AI, cutting printed stickers]

The AI became a safe “third party” they could critique together.

Conclusion · Design Guidance

This project proposes a model for emotional UX with children: design tools that act like campfires. The tool creates a shared object (a sticker, a character, a message). Children gather around it, react to it, and talk through it — instead of being put on the spot.

  • Curiosity – Surprise and play (“roll the dice,” “regenerate the sticker”) invite honest exploration.
  • Ownership – Let kids rewrite, restyle, or reject the AI’s message. Control = safety.
  • Enjoyment – Humor turns embarrassment into connection. Fun is the gateway to empathy.

As an educator, I now teach AI design for children as: not “AI that judges you,” but “AI that helps you create something you can talk about together.”
