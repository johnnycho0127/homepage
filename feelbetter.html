Emotions at Play: Designing AI & Tangible Tools for Children’s Emotional Learning

A playful emotional learning toolkit co-designed with 27 children (ages 6–12), combining tangible tools (storytelling dice, empathy games, stickers) and an AI-powered “feel-better sticker” generator.

Problem Space

Children today have fewer chances to practice empathy, peer negotiation, and emotional regulation in everyday settings. Social interaction is increasingly digital, and many emotional “lessons” are adult-scripted instead of child-driven. Our goal: design playful tools that let kids comfortably express feelings, find connection, and build empathy together — without making it feel like therapy or school.

User Research

We ran four 90-minute participatory design sessions with 27 children (ages 6–12). Each session mixed storytelling, game design, and lightweight technology use. We collected video, facilitator notes, and children’s physical artifacts (drawings, stickers, game prototypes).

  • Emotional Storytelling (PD1): Kids used giant “Inside Out” emotion dice to recall real feelings and tell stories. Many chose “negative” emotions like anger or disgust because they felt “more fun.”
  • Empathy Game Design (PD2): Small groups co-created games (“You Are Not Alone”) where players share when they felt scared, embarrassed, etc., and discover they’re not alone.
  • AI Feel-Better Stickers (PD3): Each child told a personal story, picked a feeling (sad / angry / scared / worried / lonely), and generated a comforting sticker + message using our AI tool.
  • Peer Stickers (PD4): Children made stickers for each other’s stories, then compared “how I see my feeling” vs “how my friend sees it,” which sparked perspective-taking and empathy.

Key Research Insights

  • Negative feelings were not scary — they were entertaining. Kids said things like “My favorite emotion is disgust” and “Negatives are fun,” then turned anger, guilt, and fear into jokes, stories, and rules in their games.
  • Empathy looked like ‘we’ve been there too,’ not ‘I imagine you.’ Children bonded over shared experience (“I’ve had situations like that”) instead of abstract perspective-taking.
  • Tangible > screen. Kids strongly preferred physical objects (dice, wheels, printed stickers) over purely on-screen prompts.
  • Humor lowered the barrier to vulnerability. They laughed together at “embarrassing” stories and even at AI mistakes, which made sharing feel safe instead of stressful.

Design Process

STEP 1
Explore Feelings Through Play

Emotional dice + storytelling let kids surface real experiences (fear, shame, anger) in a low-pressure way. They quickly hacked the rules and made it sillier → safer.

STEP 2
Co-Design Empathy Games

Small groups built “You Are Not Alone” games using cards, spinners, dice, and Jenga. Rules rewarded honesty, listening, and cheering for each other.

STEP 3
Prototype AI Sticker Tool

We built a web app that turns a child’s story + chosen feeling into a “feel-better” sticker, an encouraging message, and an explanation of the emotion.

STEP 4
Iterate With Peer Feedback

Kids said: some AI responses felt “corny,” “not listening,” or “doesn’t get me.” We iterated toward more editable language and more visual control before printing.

Wireframes & Interaction Flow

I created low- and mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma to define the AI sticker creation flow:

  • 1. Story Input Screen
    Child uploads a photo of their drawing, selects how they feel (sad / angry / scared / worried / lonely), and writes a short description of “what happened.”
  • 2. AI Sticker Preview Screen
    The app shows: (a) generated sticker art, (b) “feel-better” message, and (c) emotional reflection text. Children can request changes (“make it cuter,” “try again,” “add dog,” etc.).
  • 3. Print Layout Screen
    Final stickers are arranged in a printable sheet so kids can cut, trade, and keep them — turning digital reflection into something tangible they own.

These wireframes helped us quickly test tone (supportive vs. “too adult”), visual style (cute / playful / kid-owned), and control (how much can they edit?). I will include annotated screenshots in the final portfolio using frames exported from Figma:

Outcomes & Impact

Behavioral Outcome

Children openly discussed difficult feelings (anger, embarrassment, guilt) and even competed to share them, because the context felt like play, not therapy.

Social Outcome

Empathy showed up as “we’re in this together.” They validated each other through shared stories, jokes, and co-created rules — not just by saying “I understand you.”

Design Outcome

We arrived at a hybrid toolkit: physical prompts (dice, cards, stickers) + a supportive AI interface that kids could edit, joke with, critique, and ultimately make their own.

My Contribution

  • Interaction Designer: Built the AI sticker creation flow in Figma (story input → AI preview → print sheet) and defined kid-facing microcopy.

This work shows how children can co-design emotional tools — not just use them — and how playful UX can turn “hard feelings” into curiosity, agency, and connection.
