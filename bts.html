Emotions at Play: Designing AI & Tangible Tools for Children’s Emotional Learning

A participatory UX research and design project exploring how AI and playful physical tools can support children’s emotional learning. I led the full design cycle with 27 children (ages 6–12), from co-design sessions to Figma prototyping and iterative testing.

Problem Statement

  1. State of the world:
    Children are under emotional stress (anxiety, isolation, peer tension) but have fewer safe spaces to work through it together. Research shows that play — storytelling, pretending, co-inventing rules — helps kids rehearse and regulate emotions socially.
  2. The big BUT:
    Most “emotional learning” tools are adult-led and individual (“tell me how you feel”). They miss how kids actually process feelings: joking about them, acting them out, and realizing “you felt that too.” Tech and AI tools rarely support that peer-to-peer emotional work.
  3. Therefore, we did:
    We set out to build and test playful tools — physical AND AI-powered — that help kids express emotions, see that others share those emotions, and create emotional artifacts they can keep and talk about together. Our design question: Can we make technology that works like a campfire — something kids gather around to tell real stories?
Research Goal
Understand how children naturally express difficult feelings with peers, translate those behaviors into design requirements, and prototype an AI-assisted tool that supports empathy — not just self-calming.

1 · Understand

I began by mapping common emotional “pressure points” for kids: embarrassment in public, conflict with friends, fear of “getting in trouble” for reacting. I also surveyed existing social-emotional learning tools.

The pattern: most tools correct or diagnose. Very few let kids play with emotions or share them safely with peers. In contrast, prior work shows kids often process emotion socially — joking, performing, and rewriting the story with friends.

Early belief: Design shouldn’t only soothe the child. It should support the space between children.

2 · Observe (Participatory Design with Children)

PD1 · Storytelling Dice

We used oversized “emotion dice.” Kids rolled anger / fear / disgust / sadness and told a real story from their own life. They drew scenes and acted them out.

They loved so-called “negative” emotions because they felt dramatic and funny. One child joked that disgust “is my favorite emotion.” This told us: difficult feelings aren’t scary; they’re performable.

PD2 · Empathy Game (“You Are Not Alone”)

Small groups made games where you admit something stressful (“I was nervous before a presentation”) and others signal “me too.” Empathy sounded less like “I’m sorry” and more like “you’re not weird, I felt that too.”

My insight:
Kids kept creating and holding little emotional tokens — drawings, cards, characters, stickers. They wanted something physical that says “this is how I felt.”

That insight becomes the core design opportunity: emotion as an artifact.

[Insert PD1 / PD2 photos: dice, empathy game boards, early “feeling stickers” kids drew]

We learned that kids want to externalize emotion into something they can show, trade, or display.

3 · Define

We defined a core requirement:

Design requirement:
Give kids a playful, ownable emotional artifact — something they can edit, laugh about, hand to a peer, or keep for themselves. Not a score. Not advice. A thing.

We organized all the child feedback into three design pillars:

  • Curiosity – “What happens if I roll this?” “What happens if I regenerate the sticker?”
  • Ownership – “That’s not how I felt. Let me change it.”
  • Enjoyment – “That’s funny.” Laughter lowered the barrier to honesty.

These became my evaluation criteria for every UI decision after this point.

4 · Ideate

I proposed the AI Emotional Sticker Tool. Core idea:

  • Child tells a brief story about when they felt something intense (angry, scared, embarrassed).
  • Tool generates a “feel-better” sticker: an image + message.
  • Child can edit the text, regenerate visuals, and gift the sticker to themselves or a peer.

This supports what I call the Campfire Model: the sticker (not the child) becomes the “center.” Everyone can talk about it, react to it, or laugh at it without putting pressure on anyone. The tool is the shared flame.

Design philosophy shift: AI is not there to be “right.” AI is there to start a conversation.

5 · Prototype (AI Emotional Sticker Tool)

I translated the concept into mid- and hi-fidelity Figma prototypes. The flow:

  • Story Input: “Tell us what happened” + choose an emotion (sad / angry / scared / worried / lonely).
  • AI Output Preview: Sticker art + short supportive message + plain-language reflection.
  • Customize: Kids can regenerate visuals, rewrite the message, and style the sticker.
  • Print & Share: Stickers export to print sheets so they become physical, tradable objects.

I intentionally avoided “teacher voice.” Kids distrusted prescriptive tone (“Calm down”). They responded better to playful, caring tone that sounded like a friend or mascot. That became a content design rule for the UI.

6 · Test

We tested two modes: “self-sticker” (for me) and “peer-sticker” (for you). Kids compared “how I described my feeling” vs “how you described my feeling.”

That comparison created gentle emotional talk. A common response was, “I wasn’t mad, I was scared.” Correcting each other felt safe because they were talking about the sticker, not accusing a friend.

They also openly critiqued AI output: “The AI’s message is weird.” “Message needs to be improved.” “I want it to be slow.”

And they loved the physical result. One child said the sticker “makes me feel happy” because it was cute and keepable.

Design lesson:
Kids don’t want “perfect empathy” from AI. They want editable empathy.

[Insert testing photos: kids comparing “my sticker” vs “your sticker of me,” laughing at AI, cutting printed stickers]

The AI became a safe third party that they could critique together.

Conclusion · Design Guidance

This project suggests a new model for emotional UX with children: technology should act like a campfire. The tool creates a shared object (a sticker, a character, a message) that kids gather around, react to, fix, and laugh about together — instead of being evaluated alone.

  • Curiosity – Use surprise and play (emotion dice, regenerating stickers) to invite emotional exploration.
  • Ownership – Let children rewrite, restyle, and reject AI output. Control = safety.
  • Enjoyment – Humor turns embarrassment into connection. Fun is not extra; it’s the pathway to empathy.

As a designer and educator, I now treat curiosity, ownership, enjoyment as core design criteria for emotionally sensitive products. I teach AI not as an authority, but as a co-creator that invites reflection, critique, and conversation.
