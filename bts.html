UX Case Study

Emotions at Play: Designing Child-Centered AI for Emotional Support

I led the end-to-end design of a playful emotional support tool for children (ages 6–12). The product combines physical play, peer empathy, and an AI-powered “feel-better sticker” generator. This case study shows my UX process: how I scoped the problem, co-designed with real users, built and tested prototypes in Figma and code, and iterated on interaction patterns for emotional safety.

My Role
UX Research Lead & Product Designer
Participatory research, interaction design, facilitation, synthesis
Timeline

Core Skills

1. Discovery · Why this product needs to exist

Children are experiencing more stress, anxiety, and loneliness, but they’re getting fewer natural opportunities to practice empathy, negotiate conflict, or talk about feelings with peers. A lot of what’s called “emotional learning” today is adult-scripted (“tell me how you feel”) or compliance-based (“use the correct emotion word”). That model doesn’t reflect how kids actually express emotion in their lives.

My design question was:

How might we create an experience where kids can safely express hard feelings, see they’re not alone, and feel supported — without making it feel like therapy or school?

What I did in this phase

  • Background scan of child emotional development and play-based learning.
  • Identified a gap: almost no tools let kids co-create emotional meaning with each other using both physical and digital media.
  • Defined initial concept: a “play first, feelings second” journey instead of “label your feelings.”

Why this matters for UX

This is where pedagogy meets product. I’m not only studying kids; I’m modeling an experience architecture for emotionally sensitive users. This is the same mindset I would teach in a UX classroom: problem framing before solution making.

2. Generative Research with Users

I ran four participatory design sessions (“PD1–PD4”) with 27 children (ages 6–12). Each session was both a research probe and a design sprint. Instead of treating kids as “testers,” I treated them as co-designers. This gave me authentic behavioral data and immediate design direction.

Participants and Methods

  • 27 children (6–12 years old), mixed backgrounds, gender-balanced. Sessions held in small groups to support peer comfort.
  • Data sources: video recordings, facilitator notes, artifacts made by the kids (drawings, stickers, game prototypes).
  • Analysis: iterative coding in Atlas.ti; I led synthesis and theme development across sessions.

What I learned in this phase

  • Kids don’t start with “I’m sad.” They start with humor, exaggeration, performance. This tells us how the UI tone should sound.
  • They feel safer when emotions are made into a shared activity, not an interview.
  • They prefer tangible, modifiable objects (dice, cards, stickers) over static screens. That led me toward hybrid physical-digital design.

These insights drove the next step: instead of jumping straight into a slick high-fidelity app, I intentionally prototyped experiences first (storytelling games, empathy games) before prototyping interfaces. That sequencing was strategic and user-driven.

3. Prototype Sprint 1 · Emotional Storytelling with Dice (PD1)

I created giant “Inside Out”–style emotion dice and story cards. Kids rolled an emotion (anger, disgust, fear, etc.), told a story from their life that matched it, and drew it on large sticker sheets.

  • Goal: Test whether play can get kids to surface authentic emotions voluntarily.
  • Observation: Many kids said they prefer “negative” emotions because they’re more dramatic and funny (“My favorite emotion is disgust”).
  • Unexpected behavior: They modified the rules mid-activity to make it sillier (“inconvenience equals funny”), which increased sharing.

Design decision coming out of this sprint

Because humor made space for vulnerability, I realized the interface later cannot be clinical or overly “supportive teacher.” It has to sound like a playful collaborator. This insight shaped copy tone in my later Figma flows.

4. Prototype Sprint 2 · Empathy Game Co-Design (PD2)

Next, I facilitated “You Are Not Alone,” where children designed their own empathy games using cards, spinners, Jenga blocks, and challenges. They shared a real stress moment (ex: “I was nervous before public speaking”), and other players who had felt the same had to perform an action.

  • Goal: See how kids build empathy with each other.
  • Finding: Empathy was not “I imagine how you feel.” It was “Oh, me too.” That’s a totally different UX model.
  • Social dynamic: Kids were motivated by recognition and belonging, not moral instruction.

Design decision coming out of this sprint

The product shouldn’t only reflect your feeling back to you. It should also show: “Other kids have felt this, too.” That directly inspired the next phase: designing a tool that can generate visual, sharable comfort objects.

5. Product Concept · AI ‘Feel-Better Sticker’ Generator (PD3)

At this point I moved from physical/social prototyping into an actual digital product concept. I designed the first version of the AI Sticker Tool in Figma (Figma: Education AI Sticker Prototype).

Core Flow (v1 wireframe)

  1. Tell Your Story: Child uploads a quick drawing of “what happened” and picks how they feel (sad / angry / scared / worried / lonely).
  2. Generate Support: The tool generates a sticker illustration + a short “feel-better” message + a simple reflection (“It’s okay to feel angry when…”).
  3. Print & Keep: The child can print the stickers, decorate them, trade them, or keep them in personal spaces.

What I was testing in this prototype

  • Does the language feel safe / supportive / non-patronizing?
  • Do kids understand what the AI is doing with their input (transparency)?
  • Do they care about accuracy, or do they care about cuteness / ownership?

What I learned from user testing

  • Kids loved getting a physical artifact that represented their feeling. They wanted to take it home, decorate it, and show it.
  • They immediately mocked generic AI messages (“AI didn’t listen to me”, “That sounds corny”). This critique was social and funny, not hostile.
  • They wanted control: “Can I change the words?” “Can you make it cuter?” That told me this tool must be editable, not prescriptive.

Design decision for next iteration

I updated the interaction model to treat AI output as a draft to personalize — not “the answer.” In other words: AI as a co-creator the child can push back on. Pedagogically, that gives the child agency. UX-wise, that prevents the tool from sounding like a fake therapist.

6. Peer Interpretation Layer (PD4)

In the next sprint, I extended the concept: instead of making a sticker for yourself, you make one for a friend. Each child listened to another child’s story, then used the tool to generate a sticker meant to comfort that peer. Finally, they compared: “How I see me” vs “How you see me.”

  • Goal: Can this product help children practice empathy?
  • Result: Kids started talking about emotional accuracy (“That’s not how I felt”) and emotional misreading (“You thought I was mad, but I was scared”).
  • Bonus: Disagreement itself became learning. They laughed together when AI or a peer “got it wrong,” which actually opened space to clarify feelings.

Why this matters for UX

The product is no longer just self-soothing. It’s now mediating a social interaction and helping children articulate differences in interpretation. That’s a core collaborative UX pattern: shared artifact → shared reflection.

7. Interface & Technical Design

I designed the AI Sticker Tool as a lightweight web app. The interaction model visible in Figma includes three core screens:

  1. Story Input Screen
    Upload drawing → Select feeling → Type “what happened.” Design focus: gentle prompting, not interrogation.
  2. AI Preview Screen
    Displays generated sticker art + supportive message + short emotional reflection. Design focus: give the child OPTIONS (“Regenerate,” “Make it cuter,” “Change message”).
  3. Print & Personalize Screen
    Outputs a printable sticker sheet the child can physically cut, keep, or gift. Design focus: ownership, portability, and pride of creation.

From a technical mentorship perspective (what I’d teach students): we are not just “using AI.” We are shaping:

  • Prompt framing so the model reflects feelings without judgmental tone.
  • Child-safe language and guardrails (“It’s okay to feel angry when…” vs “You should calm down”).
  • Editable output so the child stays in control and the AI is positioned as a creative assistant, not an authority.

That human/AI relationship design is crucial in youth-facing products, and it’s an area I specialize in and can bring into curriculum.

8. Final Design Principles

By the end of this iterative process, three principles consistently guided my design decisions:

  • 1. Curiosity before compliance.
    Kids leaned into emotions when they were framed as playful, not evaluative. Surprise and randomness (dice, AI variations) invite exploration.
  • 2. Ownership over prescription.
    Children wanted to control the message, the visuals, and even the rules. Tools should offer editable, personalizable outputs, not “final answers.”
  • 3. Social reflection, not solo self-regulation.
    The emotional value came from peer recognition (“me too”), shared laughter, and even disagreement. UX for kids should create moments of co-experience, not isolate them.

These principles became my rubric for evaluating new features. They’re also the lenses I bring when I mentor students: usability and accessibility are necessary, but in emotionally sensitive work, relational experience design is just as critical.

9. Impact

  • 27 children co-created emotional artifacts (stickers, rules, games) they felt proud of and took home, extending reflection beyond the session.
  • The iterative process revealed how AI can act as a “third object” that kids can safely critique together, rather than as an authority.
  • The work produced a design framework (curiosity · ownership · enjoyment) that I now use to guide emotionally aware interaction design.

Practically: this work has informed how I teach user research, prototyping with vulnerable populations, and responsible AI for youth. It also demonstrates my ability to lead research-driven UX projects from discovery to functional prototypes with real users.
