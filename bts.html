Problem Statement

  1. State of the world: Children are navigating anxiety, loneliness, and conflict in increasingly digital environments. Research shows that play supports emotional growth by giving kids space to rehearse feelings, negotiate meaning, and build empathy together, especially when they create shared objects like stories or games.
  2. The big BUT: Most emotional-learning tools for youth are adult-scripted and individual (“tell me how you feel”), not co-created and social. There is limited understanding of how kids themselves repurpose playful tools — including AI — to express, reinterpret, and share emotions with peers.
  3. Therefore, we did: We set out to design and test a hybrid system — tangible activities + an AI-powered sticker tool — that helps children express emotions, see that “others feel this too,” and build empathy through playful artifacts. Our research goal: Can AI and physical play act like a ‘campfire’ that invites kids to gather, share, and co-make emotional meaning?
Research Goal
Use participatory design with children to (1) understand how they naturally express and negotiate difficult feelings through play, (2) translate those behaviors into interface requirements, and (3) build/test an AI-assisted prototype that supports peer empathy, not just self-regulation.

1 · Understand

Before making anything, I mapped current conditions: kids are emotionally overloaded (stress, conflict, anxiety), yet most “social-emotional learning” tools correct behavior instead of honoring lived feelings. Literature says play lets kids safely reframe negative emotions into something manageable and even fun.

Design implication: support honest, messy feelings — not only “positive coping.” That means the UI tone cannot sound like a school counselor.

2 · Observe (Participatory Design with Children)

PD1 · Storytelling Dice

Children rolled custom “emotion dice” (anger, fear, disgust, etc.) and told real stories based on those emotions. They loved exploring so-called “negative” emotions because those felt dramatic, funny, and honest — not shameful.

PD2 · Empathy Game (“You Are Not Alone”)

In small groups, kids designed games that rewarded admitting hard moments (“I was nervous before speaking”). Other kids said “me too,” acted it out, or built on it. Empathy happened as co-experience, not as moral lecture.

Key observation:
Children repeatedly gravitated toward stickers and other portable visual tokens as “this is how I felt.” Stickers became little emotional artifacts they wanted to keep, trade, and show.

3 · Define

From PD1 and PD2, we defined the core design requirement: Give kids a shared emotional object they can point to, laugh about, critique, edit, and keep.

  • Emotions must become visible, collectible, and tradable.
  • The artifact should invite group conversation (“that happened to me too”), not just self-reflection.
  • Adults should not own the language. Kids should.

This led directly to the “sticker as emotional artifact” concept.

4 · Ideate

I sketched interaction models where a tool could:

  • Listen to a child’s story about a tough moment.
  • Generate a “feel-better” sticker for that moment (image + message).
  • Let peers create supportive stickers for each other and compare interpretations.

Conceptually, this becomes a campfire model: the tool is not the teacher. The tool is the “fire in the middle” that everyone gathers around to tell stories, laugh, and feel seen together.

6 · Test

We tested with children using both self-stickers (“for me”) and peer-stickers (“for you”). Kids critiqued the AI’s tone (“too corny”), rewrote text, and requested visual changes (“make it cuter,” “add dog ears”). They treated AI output as something to edit — not something to obey.

This confirmed a core requirement: the child must own the message. The tool should offer drafts, not diagnoses.

Conclusion · What I Learned / Design Guidance

The project supports a new model for emotionally intelligent UX with children: tools should act less like instructors and more like campfires. Kids gather around them, contribute their own stories, remix each other’s ideas, laugh at AI’s weirdness, and negotiate meaning together.

  • Curiosity Randomness and surprise (emotion dice, unexpected AI stickers) invite children to explore even “difficult” emotions. Curiosity becomes the on-ramp to reflection.
  • Ownership Children rewrote rules, rewrote sticker text, and reshaped the tone. Emotional tools should be editable and negotiable, not prescriptive.
  • Enjoyment Fun, humor, and even “getting it wrong” together turned vulnerability into connection. Enjoyment isn’t extra; it’s the mechanism that makes empathy safe.

For my teaching: I use this project to mentor students through ethical AI design, participatory research with vulnerable users, and prototyping for social impact. The work demonstrates how design thinking can be rigorous, research-driven, and emotionally responsible.
