Emotions at Play: Designing AI & Tangible Tools for Children’s Emotional Learning

A participatory UX research and design project exploring how AI and tangible play can foster emotional intelligence in children. I led the full design cycle — from generative research and facilitated co-design sessions with 27 children (ages 6–12), to interaction design in Figma, prototyping an AI-powered “feel-better sticker” tool, and testing it iteratively with real users.

Problem Statement

  1. State of the world:
    Children today are navigating anxiety, loneliness, and social pressure in increasingly digital worlds. Shifts in socialization, limited unstructured peer time, and post-pandemic caution from adults have reduced the spaces where kids naturally learn empathy, negotiation, and emotional regulation through everyday play. Researchers and global health orgs warn that children's emotional well-being is in crisis, with growing stress and fewer safe opportunities to “practice feelings together” in low-stakes settings.
  2. The big BUT:
    Many tools meant to “teach emotions” are adult-scripted, corrective, and individual. Kids are told to identify the “right” feeling word and calm down. What’s missing is a model of emotional learning that is child-led, social, and playful — where children can experiment with feelings, laugh about mistakes, and discover that “I am not the only one who felt this.” Existing work in AI and emotion mostly focuses on specialized interventions or structured skill training, not everyday emotional co-creation between peers.
  3. Therefore, we did:
    We designed and evaluated a hybrid system — tangible games plus an AI-powered “feel-better sticker” tool — to explore how children express, share, and reinterpret emotions together. Our research goal was to ask:
    Can we design playful tools (both physical and AI-driven) that act like a campfire — a shared place where kids gather, tell real stories, trade emotional artifacts, and build empathy together?
Research Goal
Use participatory design with children to:
(1) understand how they already express and negotiate difficult feelings through play,
(2) translate those behaviors into design requirements, and
(3) build/test an AI-assisted prototype that supports peer empathy — not just self-regulation.

1 · Understand

Before making anything, I did foundational work: reviewing prior HCI and learning sciences research, interviewing facilitators who work with children, and mapping typical emotional “failure points” (conflict with peers, being embarrassed in public, fear of “getting in trouble” for reacting).

The literature is clear: kids learn emotional negotiation not by being told how to feel, but by trying it out socially — through role-play, storytelling, inside jokes, and bending rules with peers. These are rehearsal spaces where children practice self-regulation, test boundaries, and learn empathy in real time.

My early hypothesis: If we want to design for children’s emotional lives, we have to design for the space between children — not just inside the child.

That belief became the backbone of this work and shaped every UX decision I made afterward: language tone, interaction pacing, even why the final tool produces stickers you can hold instead of a score or a report card.

2 · Observe (Participatory Design with Children)

PD1 · Storytelling Dice

We created large “emotion dice” inspired by Pixar’s Inside Out. Each roll surfaced an emotion (anger, fear, disgust, embarrassment, sadness), and kids then told a real story about that feeling. They drew scenes, acted them out, and turned discomfort into something performable and funny.

Children repeatedly chose what adults would call “negative” emotions — and they loved it. One callback from the session: a participant gleefully explained that disgust was their favorite emotion because “she’s dramatic and it’s fun to talk like her.” From a UX lens, this matters: it shows emotional safety emerging not from calmness, but from performative exaggeration.

PD2 · Empathy Game (“You Are Not Alone”)

In the second session, kids co-designed empathy games in small groups. Each game started with someone sharing a real stress moment — “I was nervous before public speaking” — and other players signaling if they had felt the same, sometimes by jumping, spinning, or shouting a silly safe word.

What happened in those groups was important: empathy wasn’t delivered as “I’m sorry you went through that.” It sounded more like “Same. I felt that too. You’re not weird.” That is peer validation, not adult validation. That is the social glue.

My reflection:
Across PD1 and PD2, kids kept making and holding little artifacts — sketches of moments, token cards, even emotions-as-characters. They started treating these as emotional badges. That behavior led directly to the next design move: could we make a tool whose main output is a custom emotional artifact they can keep, trade, or gift?

3 · Define

After PD1 and PD2, we had a sharp design requirement:

Design requirement:
Give kids a playful, visual artifact that says “this is how I felt,” that they can edit, laugh at, hand to a peer, or keep for themselves — without an adult translating it.

In synthesis, three recurring qualities described how children were already doing “emotional work” for themselves and each other:

  • Curiosity – Kids probed tough feelings through experimentation and rule-bending: “What if anger could be a game?”
  • Ownership – They insisted on controlling the framing of their own emotions, inventing characters, changing the rules of the game, and defending their choices in front of peers.
  • Enjoyment – Laughter and silliness made vulnerability feel safe. Kids stayed engaged because it felt fun, not because we asked them to “open up.”

I formalized these three as evaluation pillars for every prototype that followed. This is where my teaching brain kicked in: I began treating “curiosity, ownership, enjoyment” as a rubric for emotionally intelligent UX.

4 · Ideate

I developed the core interaction concept: the AI Emotional Sticker Tool. The idea was to let a child:

  • Tell a short story about when they felt something intense (angry, scared, embarrassed, sad).
  • Generate a “feel-better” sticker — an image + message — using AI.
  • Remix that sticker for themself or gift it to a peer: “Here’s how I see your feeling.”

This model treats AI not as an authority that diagnoses emotion, but as a creative assistant that produces a starting point. When the AI message felt off, kids openly mocked it: “That’s weird. I wouldn’t say that.” Instead of shutting down, they laughed together, and then rewrote it. That critique itself became emotional reflection.

As a designer, this was a turning point: I stopped chasing “perfect” AI empathy. I started designing for editable AI — AI that invites children to disagree and improve it.

I call this the Campfire Model: the tool sits in the middle like a shared fire, generating sparks (stickers, jokes, mini characters), and kids gather around it, reacting, negotiating, co-authoring. The important thing isn’t that the tool is “right.” The important thing is that it keeps the circle together.

5 · Prototype (AI Emotional Sticker Tool)

I built mid-fidelity wireframes and then an interactive prototype in Figma for the AI Emotional Sticker Tool. The flow was intentionally simple and child-led:

  • Story Input: “Tell us what happened” + “How did you feel?” Kids could upload a quick drawing or write a short sentence.
  • AI Output Preview: The tool generated sticker art + a supportive message + a short emotion reflection (“It’s okay to feel angry when…”).
  • Customize: Children could change the wording, regenerate visuals, or add playful elements (“make it cuter,” “add dog ears”).
  • Print & Share: Stickers were laid out for print so kids could cut, trade, and keep them — taking emotional support into their real lives.

As the design matured, I deliberately pulled away from a “teacher voice.” Children mistrusted anything that sounded like moral guidance (“You should calm down”). They preferred messages that felt like a caring peer or a funny character. This is where my design philosophy around tone-as-safety really solidified.

6 · Test

We tested both “self-stickers” (you generate a sticker for your own story) and “peer-stickers” (you generate a sticker for someone else’s story). Children then compared versions: “How I described my feeling” vs “How you described my feeling.”

That comparison created powerful dialogue. Kids corrected each other gently: “I wasn’t mad, I was scared.”
Emotional misreadings actually became productive, because the artifact — the sticker — sat in the middle. No one had to directly accuse anyone. They could just point and say, “That’s not really me.”

They also critiqued the AI fearlessly, which was great. We heard: “The AI’s message is weird.” “Message needs to be improved.” “I want it to be slow.”

My reflection as a designer:
Children didn’t want “perfect” empathy from AI. They wanted editable empathy. That’s a huge design lesson for child-facing AI.

Finally, kids loved the physicality. One participant said they liked the sticker because “it was cute … it makes me feel happy,” especially if it had a friendly animal character. Tangibility gave them ownership. They could hold their feelings.

Conclusion · What I Learned & Design Guidance

This work argues for a shift in how we design “emotional support” for children: instead of focusing on diagnosis (“name the feeling, fix the feeling”), we should design campfire experiences.

The campfire model treats technology as the warm center of a circle: it generates artifacts (stickers, characters, shared jokes) that kids can gather around. Because the artifact sits in the middle, no one child is “on the spot.” The tool becomes the thing everyone looks at, critiques, laughs with — and that makes difficult emotions safe to talk about.

  • Curiosity
    When activities invited experimentation — rolling weird emotion dice, regenerating AI stickers “just to see” — kids leaned in. Curiosity was the on-ramp.
  • Ownership
    Children wanted to control how their feelings were represented. They bent rules, rewrote sticker messages, and defended “their version” of the story in front of peers. Ownership built pride and safety.
  • Enjoyment
    Fun wasn’t just entertainment — it actively turned embarrassment and fear into something survivable, even joyful. Humor made emotional honesty sustainable.

For me as a UX researcher, designer, and educator, this project crystallized a core belief: emotionally intelligent design is not about delivering the “right” message — it’s about creating social, modifiable artifacts that people can gather around.

For teaching, I now use this work to guide students through: participatory methods with vulnerable users, ethical AI as a collaborator (not authority), and tangible takeaways that travel beyond the interface (printable stickers, objects kids keep). In other words: design for after the session.
