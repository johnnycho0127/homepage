UX Case Study · Double Diamond Process

You Are Tilted!

A team-based tabletop game co-designed with youth esports players to help teams recognize, talk about, and manage tilt — intense frustration that spreads through a team and hurts performance, communication, and mental well-being.

My Role

Lead Researcher, UX Designer, Facilitator

Timeline

8 months co-design + 4 months user testing

Team

15 youth players · 7 collegiate coaches · librarians · coordinators · 5 researchers

{/* PROBLEM STATEMENT */}

Problem Statement

Background

Competitive esports teams experience tilt — a rapid spike of frustration that spreads between teammates, causes blame and hostility, and destroys team cohesion. Most existing tools (mute, block, report) treat tilt as an individual behavior or “toxicity problem,” instead of a shared emotional state.

Design Challenge

How might we design a playful, psychologically safe activity that helps esports teammates surface frustration, reset together, and build empathy — without shaming anyone for being emotional?

Research Questions

  • How does open communication, facilitated by a team-based game, enhance team cohesion?
  • What tilt-management strategies can players actually practice together (not just be told to do)?
  • What design opportunities and constraints appear when we build an emotional support tool specifically for teams?

{/* DISCOVER */}

Phase 1 Discover · Understand the problem space

Goal: Learn how players actually experience tilt, stress, and conflict. This phase focused on surfacing real emotions, not just “features people want.”

{/* SUBSECTION: Participatory Research */}

Participatory Research Sessions (1.1–1.8)

We ran 8 remote workshops with youth esports players (ages 14–15), collegiate players, coaches, librarians, and program coordinators. We asked: When do you feel “tilted”? What does it do to you? What happens to the team after that moment?

  • Players drew comics of “worst moments” in matches.
  • We mapped sources of frustration and blame inside teams.
  • We compared “healthy teammate” vs “unhealthy teammate.”

Insight:  Tilt is contagious. One person’s frustration spreads emotionally and socially to everyone.

Opportunity:  There is no safe way to talk about tilt during/after games without it sounding like blame.

{/* DISCOVER KEY TAKEAWAYS */}

Key Discover Phase Takeaways

  • Tilt is experienced as shame + anger + fear of “letting the team down,” not just anger.
  • Players want empathy from teammates, but they don’t know how to ask for it without seeming weak.
  • Everyone agreed: “We need a way to talk about this together.”
{/* DEFINE */}

Phase 2 Define · Frame the design challenge

Goal: Move from “there’s a problem” to “this is the specific design problem we’re solving — and for whom.”

{/* SUBSECTION: Tilt Scenarios + Coping */}

Tilt Scenario Mapping & Coping Workshops (Sessions 2.1–2.2)

We asked players to walk us through real “tilt moments,” minute by minute: what sparked it, how their body felt, what they said, how their teammates reacted, and what happened next.

  • We collected real coping strategies (“take a break,” “make a dumb joke so everyone laughs,” “reset comms”).
  • We also collected harmful patterns (“I shut down,” “I blame them first so I don’t get blamed”).

Insight:  Players already have emotional regulation strategies, but teammates don’t know them.

Design Goal:  Create a social space where teammates can safely share “here’s how to help me when I’m tilted.”

{/* DEFINE OUTPUT */}

Defined Problem Statement

We will design a cooperative, low-stakes game that helps esports teams practice talking about frustration, resetting together, and supporting each other — so tilt becomes a shared challenge instead of a personal failure.

Design Principles (Co-created with players)

  • Make it playful, not preachy.
  • Use humor to lower defensiveness.
  • Make emotional safety part of the rules.
  • Focus on “how can we help each other next time?”
{/* DEVELOP */}

Phase 3 Develop · Co-design, prototype, iterate

Goal: Build a playable thing. Test the tone, language, rules, pacing, emotional safety, and usefulness.

{/* SUBSECTION: Initial Prototype */}

Prototype Creation (Sessions 2.3–2.8)

Together with players, we created a physical + digital tabletop card game called You Are Tilted!

  • Situation Cards: real tilt triggers.
  • Heal Cards: “what helps me reset.”
  • Provoke Cards: raise the stakes to practice calm in harder conditions.

Why cards?  Cards give distance. Players can talk about “the card” instead of accusing each other directly.

{/* SUBSECTION: Refinement */}

Iteration & Refinement (Sessions 3.1–3.5)

We refined the game with esports coaches, librarians, and a pro designer. We focused on clarity, flow, and accessibility.

  • Simplified rules for faster onboarding.
  • Added end-of-game reflection prompts (“What did you learn about each other?”).
  • Added collaborative win conditions (less “I won,” more “we made it through”).
  • Adjusted typography & color contrast for better readability.

Key UX Move:  We reframed the tone from “fix yourself” to “support each other.”

{/* DEVELOP SUMMARY */}

Develop Phase Takeaways

  • The game became a safe “translator” so players could say things like “I need space when I’m tilted” without embarrassment.
  • Humor (“roasting the card,” not the person) consistently lowered defenses and bonded teams.
  • Players started suggesting their own coping cards — “custom cards” became a requested feature.
{/* DELIVER */}

Phase 4 Deliver · Test with real teams, measure impact

Goal: Observe how actual competitive teams use the game, what changes in their communication, and what still breaks.

{/* USER TESTING */}

User Testing (4 Rounds)

We ran 4 play sessions (50 min gameplay + survey + 15 min team debrief) with 17 collegiate esports players (ages 19–29). We recorded voice, behavior, body language, and post-game reflections.

  • “Ah-Ha” moments: teammates learned new coping styles from each other (“petting my dog calms me down”).
  • Shared humor: joking about cards created relief and psychological safety.
  • Reset strategy: teams practiced saying “let’s reset” instead of silently tilting or flaming.
  • Empathy: players reframed tilt as “our situation,” not “your problem.”

Quote:  “It’s helpful just knowing how you want to be helped. I didn’t know that before.”

{/* IMPACT + NEXT ITERATIONS */}

Design Impact

  • The game functioned as an emotional “translator” inside teams.
  • Players reported feeling more comfortable naming stress in front of each other.
  • Teams began using “reset” and “zero-zero mindset” as shared language for recovery.

Iteration Opportunities

  • Add customizable blank cards so each team can add their own coping strategies and inside jokes.
  • Shift scoring from “who wins” to “did we get through this together.”
  • Consider lightweight digital companion to track team-defined “support rules.”

{/* REFLECTION / CONCLUSION */}

Reflection & Next Steps

What I Learned as a Designer

  • Emotional safety is a design requirement. You have to design for it explicitly, not hope it happens.
  • Humor is not a “nice to have.” It was core to creating a safe channel for honesty.
  • Teams want tools that feel like “us,” not generic wellness advice, which is why customization matters.

Why This Matters

This work shows that UX for mental health doesn't have to be an app, dashboard, or chatbot. It can be a shared activity that builds empathy, shared language, and recovery habits the team can actually use under stress.

“You Are Tilted!” helped teams practice saying:  “I’m tilted, here’s how to help me — and I’ll do the same for you.”
